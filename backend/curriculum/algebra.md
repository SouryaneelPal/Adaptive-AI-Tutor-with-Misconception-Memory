# Algebra — Linear Equations

## Prerequisites
- Arithmetic with integers (positive and negative numbers)
- Order of operations (BODMAS/PEMDAS)
- Understanding of variables as unknown quantities

## Definition
An equation states that two expressions are equal. A linear equation has a variable (usually x) raised only to the power of 1 (no x², no x³).

Goal: Find the value of the variable that makes both sides equal.

The Golden Rule of Algebra: Whatever you do to one side of the equation, you MUST do exactly the same to the other side. The equation stays balanced like a scale.

## Variables and Expressions
A **variable** is a letter that represents an unknown number.
An **expression** is a combination of numbers, variables, and operations (e.g., 3x + 5).
An **equation** is an expression set equal to something (e.g., 3x + 5 = 14).

## One-Step Equations

### Adding/Subtracting to isolate x
To cancel addition, subtract. To cancel subtraction, add.

Example: x + 7 = 12
Subtract 7 from both sides: x + 7 − 7 = 12 − 7 → x = 5
Check: 5 + 7 = 12 ✓

Example: x − 4 = 9
Add 4 to both sides: x − 4 + 4 = 9 + 4 → x = 13
Check: 13 − 4 = 9 ✓

### Multiplying/Dividing to isolate x
To cancel multiplication, divide. To cancel division, multiply.

Example: 5x = 35
Divide both sides by 5: 5x/5 = 35/5 → x = 7
Check: 5 × 7 = 35 ✓

Example: x/3 = 8
Multiply both sides by 3: (x/3) × 3 = 8 × 3 → x = 24
Check: 24/3 = 8 ✓

**Common misconception**: Students add instead of subtract (doing the same operation as what's already there instead of the inverse). The inverse undoes: + undoes −, × undoes ÷.

## Two-Step Equations
Apply inverse operations in REVERSE order of BODMAS. The equation was built outward (operations applied to x last are undone first).

Step 1: Undo addition or subtraction (the outermost operation)
Step 2: Undo multiplication or division

Example: 3x + 5 = 14
Step 1: Subtract 5 from both sides: 3x = 9
Step 2: Divide both sides by 3: x = 3
Check: 3(3) + 5 = 9 + 5 = 14 ✓

Example: 2x − 7 = 11
Step 1: Add 7 to both sides: 2x = 18
Step 2: Divide both sides by 2: x = 9
Check: 2(9) − 7 = 18 − 7 = 11 ✓

Example: x/4 + 3 = 7
Step 1: Subtract 3: x/4 = 4
Step 2: Multiply by 4: x = 16
Check: 16/4 + 3 = 4 + 3 = 7 ✓

**Common misconception**: Dividing before subtracting in a two-step equation (wrong order). Always undo addition/subtraction first (outermost layer), then multiplication/division (inner layer).

## Equations with Variables on Both Sides
Move all variable terms to one side, all number terms to the other.

Example: 5x + 3 = 3x + 11
Step 1: Subtract 3x from both sides: 2x + 3 = 11
Step 2: Subtract 3 from both sides: 2x = 8
Step 3: Divide by 2: x = 4
Check: 5(4) + 3 = 23; 3(4) + 11 = 23 ✓

Example: 7x − 4 = 2x + 16
Subtract 2x: 5x − 4 = 16
Add 4: 5x = 20
Divide by 5: x = 4

**Common misconception**: Subtracting the larger x term from the smaller (resulting in negative coefficient) and then getting confused. It's fine to have a negative coefficient — just divide by the negative number at the end.

## Distributive Property in Equations
Expand brackets first: a(b + c) = ab + ac

Example: 2(x + 5) = 18
Expand: 2x + 10 = 18
Subtract 10: 2x = 8
Divide by 2: x = 4
Check: 2(4 + 5) = 2(9) = 18 ✓

Example: 3(2x − 1) = 15
Expand: 6x − 3 = 15
Add 3: 6x = 18
Divide by 6: x = 3

**Common misconception**: Only multiplying the first term inside the bracket: 2(x + 5) = 2x + 5 (WRONG). The 2 must multiply EVERY term inside: 2x + 10.

## Negative Numbers in Equations
Remember: subtracting a negative = adding a positive. Multiplying/dividing by a negative flips the sign.

Example: −3x = 12
Divide by −3: x = −4
Check: −3(−4) = 12 ✓

Example: x + (−5) = 3 is the same as x − 5 = 3, so x = 8.

**Common misconception**: −3x = 12 → x = 4 (forgetting the negative sign on the divisor). Always divide by the FULL coefficient including its sign.

## Writing Equations from Word Problems
Key phrases:
- "is", "equals", "gives", "results in" → =
- "sum", "more than", "added to", "increased by" → +
- "difference", "less than", "subtracted from", "decreased by" → −
- "product", "times", "of" → ×
- "quotient", "divided by", "per", "ratio" → ÷
- "a number", "some number" → variable (x)

Example: "Five more than three times a number is twenty."
Three times a number: 3x
Five more than: 3x + 5
Is twenty: 3x + 5 = 20
Solve: 3x = 15 → x = 5

**Common misconception**: "4 less than x" written as 4 − x instead of x − 4. "Less than" reverses the order. Read carefully: "4 less than x" means start with x and subtract 4.

## Checking Solutions
Always substitute your answer back into the original equation to verify.

If left side ≠ right side after substitution, recheck every step from the beginning.

## Common Misconceptions Summary
1. **Moving terms without changing sign**: When you move a term across =, its sign changes (add becomes subtract, etc.).
2. **Wrong order in two-step equations**: Undo addition/subtraction before multiplication/division.
3. **Partial distribution**: 2(x + 3) ≠ 2x + 3. Must multiply ALL terms in the bracket.
4. **Forgetting the negative sign in the coefficient**: −3x = 12 → x = −4, not 4.
5. **"Less than" reversal**: "4 less than x" is x − 4, not 4 − x.

## Grading Rubric — One-Step Equations
CORRECT: Applies inverse operation to both sides; isolates variable correctly.
PARTIAL: Correct method but arithmetic error.
WRONG: Adds instead of subtracts to isolate variable; applies operation to only one side; moves number without changing sign.

## Grading Rubric — Two-Step Equations
CORRECT: Undoes addition/subtraction first, then multiplication/division; applies to both sides.
PARTIAL: Correct first step but arithmetic error in second.
WRONG: Divides before subtracting (wrong order); applies operation to wrong side; only performs one step.

## Grading Rubric — Distributive Property
CORRECT: Multiplies factor by EVERY term inside brackets: 3(x+4) = 3x+12.
PARTIAL: Distributed correctly but arithmetic error on one term.
WRONG: Only multiplies first term: 3(x+4) = 3x+4; adds instead of multiplies.

## Grading Rubric — Variables on Both Sides
CORRECT: Collects all variable terms on one side, all constants on the other; solves resulting equation.
PARTIAL: Correctly moved variables but arithmetic error in combining.
WRONG: Moves variable term without changing sign; combines variable and constant terms (e.g., x+3 = 3x).

## Worked Example — Two-Step Equation
**Problem**: Solve 3x − 5 = 16
Step 1: Add 5 to both sides → 3x = 21
Step 2: Divide both sides by 3 → x = 7
Check: 3(7) − 5 = 21 − 5 = 16 ✓
**Answer: x = 7**

## Worked Example — Variables on Both Sides
**Problem**: Solve 5x + 2 = 2x + 14
Step 1: Subtract 2x from both sides → 3x + 2 = 14
Step 2: Subtract 2 from both sides → 3x = 12
Step 3: Divide by 3 → x = 4
Check: 5(4)+2 = 22; 2(4)+14 = 22 ✓
**Answer: x = 4**

## Worked Example — Distributive Property
**Problem**: Solve 2(x + 5) = 18
Step 1: Expand: 2x + 10 = 18
Step 2: Subtract 10: 2x = 8
Step 3: Divide by 2: x = 4
Check: 2(4+5) = 2×9 = 18 ✓
**Answer: x = 4**

## Worked Example — Negative Coefficient
**Problem**: Solve −3x + 6 = 18
Step 1: Subtract 6: −3x = 12
Step 2: Divide by −3: x = −4
Check: −3(−4)+6 = 12+6 = 18 ✓
**Answer: x = −4**
