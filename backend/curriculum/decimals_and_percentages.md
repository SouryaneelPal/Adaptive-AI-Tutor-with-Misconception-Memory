# Decimals and Percentages

## Prerequisites
- Place value with whole numbers
- Basic fractions (numerator and denominator)
- Multiplication and division by 10, 100, 1000

## Decimal Place Value

### Definition
A decimal point separates the whole number part from the fractional part.

Place values to the LEFT of the decimal point: ones, tens, hundreds, thousands...
Place values to the RIGHT: tenths (1/10), hundredths (1/100), thousandths (1/1000)...

Example: 34.567
- 3 = tens (30)
- 4 = ones (4)
- 5 = tenths (5/10 = 0.5)
- 6 = hundredths (6/100 = 0.06)
- 7 = thousandths (7/1000 = 0.007)

**Common misconception**: Thinking 0.7 < 0.35 because 35 > 7. WRONG. 0.7 = 0.70 (70 hundredths), and 0.35 = 35 hundredths. So 0.7 > 0.35. Always compare digits column by column, starting from the tenths place.

## Comparing Decimals
Line up the decimal points. Add zeros to make the same number of decimal places. Then compare digit by digit from left to right.

Example: Compare 0.4 and 0.38.
Write as 0.40 and 0.38.
Both have 4 and 3 in the tenths column → 4 > 3, so 0.4 > 0.38.

Example: Compare 2.5 and 2.50.
These are identical. 2.5 = 2.50 = 2.500.

## Adding and Subtracting Decimals
Line up the decimal points. Add zeros if needed to make equal length. Then add or subtract normally.

Example: 3.7 + 1.45
  3.70
+ 1.45
------
  5.15

Example: 5.2 − 2.86
  5.20
− 2.86
------
  2.34

**Common misconception**: Not lining up decimal points (right-aligning numbers instead). You must align DECIMAL POINTS, not the rightmost digit.

## Multiplying Decimals
Multiply as whole numbers first (ignore the decimal points). Then count the total decimal places in BOTH numbers and place that many from the right in the answer.

Example: 2.4 × 1.3
Multiply 24 × 13 = 312
Count decimal places: 2.4 has 1, 1.3 has 1 → total 2 decimal places
Answer: 3.12

Example: 0.05 × 0.3
Multiply 5 × 3 = 15
Decimal places: 2 + 1 = 3
Answer: 0.015 (place decimal 3 from right → 0.015)

**Common misconception**: Counting decimal places wrong or placing the decimal incorrectly. Tip: check your answer makes sense. 0.05 × 0.3 should be much smaller than 1, so 0.015 makes sense; 0.15 or 1.5 would not.

## Dividing Decimals
To divide by a decimal, convert the divisor to a whole number by multiplying both numbers by 10, 100, etc.

Example: 4.8 ÷ 0.3
Multiply both by 10: 48 ÷ 3 = 16

Example: 2.5 ÷ 0.05
Multiply both by 100: 250 ÷ 5 = 50

**Common misconception**: Moving the decimal in only one number. You MUST multiply BOTH dividend and divisor by the same power of 10.

## Converting Between Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages

### Fraction → Decimal
Divide numerator by denominator.
1/4 = 1 ÷ 4 = 0.25
3/8 = 3 ÷ 8 = 0.375
1/3 = 0.333... (recurring)

### Decimal → Fraction
Read the decimal using place value, then simplify.
0.6 = 6/10 = 3/5
0.25 = 25/100 = 1/4
0.125 = 125/1000 = 1/8

### Decimal → Percentage
Multiply by 100 (or move decimal point 2 places right).
0.45 → 45%
0.7 → 70%
1.25 → 125%

### Percentage → Decimal
Divide by 100 (or move decimal point 2 places left).
75% → 0.75
8% → 0.08
130% → 1.30

### Fraction → Percentage
Convert to decimal first, then to percentage.
3/4 = 0.75 = 75%
2/5 = 0.4 = 40%

**Common misconception**: Moving the decimal point only one place when converting decimal to percentage. You must move TWO places (multiply by 100, not 10). 0.35 = 35%, not 3.5%.

## Percentage Basics

### Definition
"Percent" means "per hundred." 40% means 40 out of every 100.

Percentages can exceed 100%: 150% means 150 out of 100, i.e., 1.5 times the original.

### Finding a Percentage of an Amount
Method: Convert percentage to decimal, then multiply.

Example: Find 30% of 80.
30% = 0.30
0.30 × 80 = 24

Example: Find 17.5% of 200.
0.175 × 200 = 35

### Finding What Percentage One Number is of Another
Formula: (Part / Whole) × 100

Example: 18 is what percentage of 72?
(18 / 72) × 100 = 0.25 × 100 = 25%

Example: 45 out of 60 students passed. What percentage passed?
(45/60) × 100 = 75%

**Common misconception**: Dividing Whole by Part instead of Part by Whole. Always: PART on top, WHOLE on bottom.

## Percentage Increase and Decrease

### Percentage Increase
New amount = Original × (1 + percentage/100)
Or: find the increase amount, then add to original.

Example: Increase £80 by 15%.
Method 1: £80 × 1.15 = £92
Method 2: 15% of 80 = £12; 80 + 12 = £92

### Percentage Decrease
New amount = Original × (1 − percentage/100)

Example: Decrease 200 kg by 30%.
200 × 0.70 = 140 kg

### Finding Percentage Change
Percentage change = (Change / Original) × 100

Example: Price went from £50 to £65. Percentage increase?
Change = 65 − 50 = 15
(15/50) × 100 = 30% increase

Example: Value fell from 400 to 300. Percentage decrease?
Change = 400 − 300 = 100
(100/400) × 100 = 25% decrease

**Common misconception**: Dividing the change by the NEW value instead of the ORIGINAL value. The formula always uses the ORIGINAL (starting) value as the denominator.

**Common misconception**: When a price increases by 10% then decreases by 10%, students say the price is back to the original. WRONG. The decrease is 10% of the HIGHER price, which is more than 10% of the original. Example: £100 → +10% → £110 → −10% → £99 (not £100).

## Reverse Percentages (Finding the Original)
Used when you know a percentage of the original, and want to find the original.

Example: After a 20% increase, a price is £144. What was the original price?
£144 = 120% of original (the original is 100%, increase adds 20%)
Original = £144 / 1.20 = £120

Example: After a 15% discount, an item costs £68. What was the original?
£68 = 85% of original
Original = £68 / 0.85 = £80

**Common misconception**: Finding 20% of £144 and subtracting (£144 − £28.80 = £115.20). WRONG. That finds 20% of the increased price, not the original. Always divide by the percentage multiplier.

## Common Misconceptions Summary
1. **Comparing decimals by length**: 0.7 > 0.35 because 7 tenths > 35 hundredths. Compare place by place.
2. **Not aligning decimal points**: Always line up the decimal point, not the right edge.
3. **Decimal to percent: move one place**: Must move TWO places (×100). 0.35 = 35%, not 3.5%.
4. **Percentage change uses new value**: Always divide by the ORIGINAL value.
5. **Reverse percentage: subtract %**: Must divide by the multiplier, not subtract the percentage from the result.
6. **+10% then −10% = original**: No — each percentage applies to a different base.
