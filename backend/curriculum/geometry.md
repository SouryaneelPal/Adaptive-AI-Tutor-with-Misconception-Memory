# Geometry — Shapes, Angles, and Area

## Prerequisites
- Basic arithmetic and fractions
- Multiplication and squaring numbers
- Understanding of measurement (length, units)

## Angles

### Definition
An angle is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint (vertex). Measured in degrees (°).

### Types of Angles
- **Acute**: less than 90°
- **Right**: exactly 90° (marked with a small square)
- **Obtuse**: between 90° and 180°
- **Straight**: exactly 180° (a straight line)
- **Reflex**: between 180° and 360°

### Angle Relationships
**Complementary angles**: two angles that add up to 90°
Example: 35° and 55° are complementary (35 + 55 = 90)

**Supplementary angles**: two angles that add up to 180°
Example: 110° and 70° are supplementary (110 + 70 = 180)

**Vertically opposite angles**: when two lines cross, opposite angles are equal.
Example: If one angle is 65°, the vertically opposite angle is also 65°.

**Angles on a straight line**: all angles on one side of a straight line add up to 180°.
**Angles around a point**: all angles around a single point add up to 360°.

**Common misconception**: Students confuse complementary (adds to 90) with supplementary (adds to 180). Memory tip: C comes before S, 90 comes before 180.

## Triangles

### Definition
A triangle is a closed shape with exactly 3 straight sides and 3 angles.

### Angle Sum Rule
The three interior angles of ANY triangle always add up to 180°.

Example: A triangle has angles 45°, 75°, and x. Find x.
45 + 75 + x = 180
120 + x = 180
x = 60°

Example: A right triangle has one angle of 35°. Find the third angle.
90 + 35 + x = 180
x = 55°

**Common misconception**: Students say triangle angles add to 360°. This is the rule for angles AROUND A POINT, not a triangle. Triangle angles ALWAYS total 180°.

### Types of Triangles by Sides
- **Equilateral**: all 3 sides equal → all 3 angles = 60°
- **Isosceles**: 2 sides equal → the 2 base angles (opposite the equal sides) are equal
- **Scalene**: all 3 sides different → all 3 angles different

Example: Isosceles triangle with apex angle 40°. Find the base angles.
Base angles are equal. Let each base angle = b.
40 + b + b = 180
40 + 2b = 180
2b = 140
b = 70°

**Common misconception**: In an isosceles triangle, students add the two equal sides instead of recognising the two BASE angles are equal. The equal angles are opposite the equal sides.

### Area of a Triangle
Area = (1/2) × base × height

IMPORTANT: The height must be PERPENDICULAR (at a right angle) to the base. It is not always the slanted side.

Example: Triangle with base = 8 cm, perpendicular height = 5 cm.
Area = 1/2 × 8 × 5 = 20 cm²

Example: Right triangle with legs 6 cm and 9 cm.
In a right triangle, the two legs are perpendicular to each other, so one is the base and one is the height.
Area = 1/2 × 6 × 9 = 27 cm²

**Common misconception**: Using the slant side as the height instead of the perpendicular height. The height must form a 90° angle with the base.

## Quadrilaterals

### Definition
A quadrilateral has 4 straight sides and 4 angles. All interior angles sum to 360°.

### Types
- **Rectangle**: opposite sides equal, all angles 90°
- **Square**: all sides equal, all angles 90°
- **Parallelogram**: opposite sides equal and parallel, opposite angles equal
- **Trapezium/Trapezoid**: exactly one pair of parallel sides
- **Rhombus**: all sides equal, opposite angles equal

### Area Formulas
- Rectangle: Area = length × width
- Square: Area = side²
- Parallelogram: Area = base × perpendicular height (NOT the slant side)
- Trapezium: Area = 1/2 × (sum of parallel sides) × perpendicular height
  = 1/2 × (a + b) × h

Example: Trapezium with parallel sides 6 cm and 10 cm, height 4 cm.
Area = 1/2 × (6 + 10) × 4 = 1/2 × 16 × 4 = 32 cm²

Example: Parallelogram with base 7 cm and perpendicular height 3 cm.
Area = 7 × 3 = 21 cm²

**Common misconception**: For a parallelogram, using the slant side as the height. The height must be the perpendicular distance between the parallel bases.

## Circles

### Key Terms
- **Radius (r)**: distance from centre to edge
- **Diameter (d)**: distance across through the centre = 2r
- **Circumference**: the distance around the circle
- **π (pi)**: approximately 3.14159 — the ratio of circumference to diameter

### Formulas
Circumference = 2πr = πd
Area = πr²

Example: Circle with radius 5 cm.
Circumference = 2 × π × 5 = 10π ≈ 31.4 cm
Area = π × 5² = 25π ≈ 78.5 cm²

Example: Circle with diameter 12 cm. Find the area.
Radius = 12/2 = 6 cm
Area = π × 6² = 36π ≈ 113.1 cm²

**Common misconception**: Using the diameter instead of the radius in the area formula. Area = πr² NOT πd². Always halve the diameter to get the radius before squaring.

**Common misconception**: Confusing circumference (one-dimensional, the boundary length) with area (two-dimensional, the space inside). Circumference uses r (or d); area uses r².

## Perimeter

### Definition
Perimeter = total distance around the outside of a shape. Sum of all side lengths.

Example: Rectangle 8 cm × 5 cm.
Perimeter = 8 + 5 + 8 + 5 = 26 cm (or 2 × (8 + 5) = 26 cm)

Example: Equilateral triangle with side 6 cm.
Perimeter = 3 × 6 = 18 cm

**Common misconception**: Confusing perimeter (add all sides) with area (multiply). A shape with a large area can have a small perimeter and vice versa.

## Polygon Angle Sums
For any polygon with n sides, the sum of interior angles = (n − 2) × 180°

- Triangle (n=3): (3−2) × 180 = 180°
- Quadrilateral (n=4): (4−2) × 180 = 360°
- Pentagon (n=5): (5−2) × 180 = 540°
- Hexagon (n=6): (6−2) × 180 = 720°

Regular polygon: all sides and angles equal. Each interior angle = [(n−2) × 180] / n

## Common Misconceptions Summary
1. **Triangle angles sum to 360°**: WRONG — 180° for triangles, 360° for quadrilaterals/angles around a point.
2. **Using slant height instead of perpendicular height**: Heights in area formulas must be perpendicular.
3. **Using diameter in the area formula**: Area = πr². Use radius, not diameter.
4. **Confusing perimeter and area**: Perimeter = boundary length; area = space enclosed.
5. **Complementary vs supplementary**: Complementary = 90°, supplementary = 180°.

## Grading Rubric — Angles in a Triangle
CORRECT: States angles sum to 180°; subtracts known angles to find missing angle.
PARTIAL: Correct method but arithmetic error.
WRONG: Uses 360° instead of 180°; says angles sum to 90°.

## Grading Rubric — Area of a Triangle
CORRECT: Area = ½ × base × perpendicular height; uses correct height; correct units².
PARTIAL: Correct formula but forgot to halve (base × height only); correct calculation but wrong units.
WRONG: Uses slant height instead of perpendicular height; uses perimeter formula.

## Grading Rubric — Area of a Circle
CORRECT: Area = πr²; correctly identifies radius (not diameter); correct units².
PARTIAL: Correct formula but used diameter instead of radius.
WRONG: Uses diameter as radius (off by factor of 4); uses circumference formula (2πr); forgets to square radius.

## Grading Rubric — Perimeter
CORRECT: Adds all side lengths; uses correct linear units (not squared).
PARTIAL: Added correctly but missed one side.
WRONG: Multiplies sides (area formula); uses squared units for perimeter.

## Worked Example — Missing Angle in Triangle
**Problem**: Triangle has angles 65° and 75°. Find the third.
Step 1: Angles in a triangle sum to 180°.
Step 2: 65 + 75 + x = 180 → 140 + x = 180 → x = 40°
**Answer: 40°**

## Worked Example — Area of a Triangle
**Problem**: Base = 10 cm, perpendicular height = 6 cm.
Area = ½ × 10 × 6 = ½ × 60 = **30 cm²**
Use the perpendicular height — not the slant side.

## Worked Example — Area of a Circle
**Problem**: Circle with radius 7 cm. (π ≈ 3.14)
Area = π × r² = 3.14 × 49 = **153.86 cm²**
If given diameter = 14, first find radius = 14 ÷ 2 = 7.

## Worked Example — Perimeter of a Rectangle
**Problem**: Rectangle 9 m × 4 m.
P = 2 × (9 + 4) = 2 × 13 = **26 m**
Perimeter uses metres (not m²) — it is a length, not an area.
